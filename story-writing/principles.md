# Core Principles

## Learn by Writing

- Do not wait to understand every theory book before writing.
- Use simple principles that can be applied immediately.
- Treat rules as tools for generating better drafts, not as reasons to freeze.

## What a Story Must Do

A strong story does three things:

- make the audience care
- create rises and falls around what they care about
- deliver emotional release at the end

If a draft does not do these, polish will not rescue it.

## Three Main Ways to Make the Audience Care

Use one or more at the opening:

- a character the audience likes or feels for
- a goal or situation the audience can worry about
- a story question the audience wants answered

These do not need equal weight, but at least one must become active quickly.

## Central Dramatic Question

Phrase the story's core as:

`Can the protagonist ___ ?`

Then inspect it on three layers:

- event layer: what concrete thing must happen
- emotional layer: what relationship or feeling is at stake
- value layer: what belief, lesson, or human meaning is being tested

Stories with only the event layer often feel thin.

## Character Appeal

To make a character easier to follow, use some of these levers:

- unfair treatment
- visible kindness or goodwill
- competence or special ability
- vulnerability
- unrealized potential

The goal is not “nice character”; the goal is “character the audience wants to keep following.”

## Structure Is About Movement, Not Decoration

- Structure exists to control attention and emotion.
- Do not use structure vocabulary as empty labels.
- Every structural turn should change what the audience expects next.

## Five Core Beats

For short-form storytelling, a reduced five-beat model is usually enough:

1. catalyst / inciting disruption
2. progress and complications
3. major setback or reversal
4. final push / climax
5. release / ending state

This is often more usable than memorizing a long beat list.

## Three-Act and 起承转合

These are framing tools, not prisons.

- Three-act helps when you need large-scale progression.
- 起承转合 helps when you want a simpler creative rhythm.
- “转” matters because it forces the story away from a straight line.

Choose the model that best helps you make decisions.

## Scene Principle

Every scene should change at least one of these:

- information
- power
- emotion
- relationship
- tactical direction

If none of them change, the scene is likely dead or redundant.

## Dialogue Principle

- Dialogue serves story.
- If the scene is weak, beautiful lines will not fix it.
- Many dialogue problems are really scene-structure problems.

## Drafting Bias

- Favor clarity over ornamental complexity.
- Favor live conflict over descriptive setup.
- Favor active choices over passive explanation.
- Favor revision that strengthens the audience question over revision that only makes prose “nicer.”
