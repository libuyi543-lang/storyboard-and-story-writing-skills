# Scene and Dialogue

## Scene Design

Treat a scene as a dramatic engine, not a container for information.

For every scene, define:

- what the scene is trying to do in the whole story
- who wants what right now
- what resists that desire
- what turns by the end

If the scene has no turn, it usually needs redesign.

## Single-Scene Skeleton

Use this template:

1. audience enters with a live expectation
2. protagonist pursues a tactic
3. opposing force blocks or redirects
4. pressure rises
5. a turn changes the meaning of the scene
6. the ending creates a new expectation

## Scene Questions

Before drafting lines, answer:

- Why this scene now?
- Why is this the next scene rather than a summary?
- What new thing happens emotionally or strategically?
- What would be missing if the scene were removed?

## Writing Dialogue

Start from these rules:

- dialogue is not the scene; it is one tool inside the scene
- subtext matters only if the dramatic situation is already clear
- “good lines” do not save weak conflict
- lines gain meaning from context, relationship, and timing

## Dialogue Workflow

1. Build the scene conflict first.
2. Identify what each speaker wants from the exchange.
3. Identify what each speaker is hiding, avoiding, or misreading.
4. Draft blunt functional dialogue if necessary.
5. Refine for tone, personality, compression, and subtext.

## When Dialogue Feels Bad

Assume story problems before language problems.

Common causes:

- the scene has no real conflict
- both characters want the same thing in the same way
- the scene is explaining instead of moving
- the turn arrives too late or not at all
- the line tries to sound “good” instead of doing work

## Dialogue Checks

- Can the audience infer context without over-explaining?
- Does each speaker have a distinct relation to the scene goal?
- Is at least one line changing the pressure?
- Could any long explanation become action, behavior, or omission?

## Climax Scene Checks

When writing the climax, ask:

- does the protagonist make the decisive choice?
- is the hardest pressure active here?
- does the scene answer the central question on event, emotion, and value levels?
- does the aftermath make the result land?
