# Core Principles

## Story First

- Start from scene function, not camera variety.
- Ask what the audience must understand, feel, and anticipate by the end of the beat.
- Collapse dialogue into beats before designing shots.

## Main Shot Before Coverage

- For each beat, find the one image that best carries the beat's meaning.
- Treat that image as the beat's main shot.
- Add extra shots only when they reveal reaction, clarify geography, redirect attention, or improve rhythm.

## Axis and the Five-Position Baseline

- In a simple two-person scene, use shot-reverse-shot and the five-position baseline as the default grammar:
  two over-shoulders,
  two singles,
  one side two-shot.
- Keep cameras on one side of the axis unless another goal is more important.
- If you break axis, justify it through one of these bridges:
  movement,
  a neutral/on-axis shot,
  a strong eyeline bridge,
  a viewpoint shift,
  or a rhythm/meaning gain that outweighs continuity loss.

## Derive Camera From Image

- Decide what the audience should see first.
- Then place the camera that can produce that image.
- Avoid collecting angles first and inventing meaning later.

## Shot Size

- Choose shot size according to what information the beat needs:
  expression and reaction -> tighter,
  body action and blocking -> wider,
  relationship in space -> wider or two-shot.
- Avoid same-size same-composition back-to-back cuts.
- Avoid drawer-pulling: wide -> tight -> wide -> tight without purpose.
- Use gradual tightening or loosening to create escalation, release, or smoothness.

## Camera Height and Angle

- Choose height and angle for power, intimacy, and viewpoint, not decoration.
- Use height shifts when status, pressure, vulnerability, or audience alignment changes.
- Keep height stable when the scene needs neutrality or invisible continuity.

## Composition and Focus

- Composition does two jobs:
  make the frame readable,
  and express a precise meaning.
- Think beyond rules like rule-of-thirds.
- Use symmetry, asymmetry, emptiness, crowding, depth, and foreground/background layering to express emotional meaning.
- Always know where the viewer's eye should land first.

## Space

- Space becomes readable through:
  perspective,
  overlap,
  foreground/midground/background separation,
  scale contrast,
  and motivated movement.
- A storyboard should let a reader reconstruct geography without needing extra explanation.

## Movement and Blocking

- Move characters or camera when story state changes.
- Use staging to hand off focus between characters.
- Use movement to reveal space, justify reframing, or transfer energy across a cut.

## Viewpoint

- Decide whose side the audience stands on.
- To bind the audience to a character, combine:
  what the character sees,
  and the character's reaction.
- To stay objective, avoid repeatedly privileging one character's look-reaction chain.

## Rhythm and Paragraphing

- Rhythm is built by shot duration, visual density, motion continuity, and cut pattern.
- Group shots into clear paragraphs when the scene changes tactic, emotion, or information.
- A sequence can become clearer when one paragraph is smooth and the next is intentionally sharp or broken.

## Montage

- Montage is useful when the meaning comes from shot adjacency, not from one isolated shot.
- Use it to build irony, inference, compression, contrast, or thematic force.
