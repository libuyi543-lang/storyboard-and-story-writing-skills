# Scene Workflow

## 1. Read the Scene as Drama

Extract these items before designing shots:

- scene purpose in the larger story
- who wants what
- obstacle or counterforce
- emotional turn
- key reveal or image
- final audience takeaway

Write a one-sentence summary:

`This scene is about A trying to get B to ___, but ___, and by the end the audience realizes/feels ___ .`

## 2. Turn the Scene Into Beats

Group dialogue and action into beats.

A beat should change at least one of:

- information
- power balance
- emotional temperature
- physical relationship
- tactic

If several lines do not change any of those, keep them inside one beat.

## 3. Choose Viewpoint

Pick one:

- objective
- attached to character A
- attached to character B
- shifting on purpose

State the choice explicitly.
If viewpoint shifts, note exactly where and why.

## 4. Draw the Spatial Map

Mark:

- entrances/exits
- relative positions
- movement paths
- active axis
- important props or obstacles

For moving scenes, mark where a new local axis appears.

## 5. Find the Main Shot of Each Beat

For each beat, ask:

- If only one shot could remain, which shot best communicates the beat?
- What must be visible in that shot?
- Which character, object, or direction owns the frame?

This becomes the beat's main shot.

## 6. Expand Only as Needed

Add supporting shots only when they solve one of these problems:

- reveal a reaction that changes meaning
- clarify where people are
- show a crucial physical action
- carry the audience's eye into the next cut
- create a stronger rhythm or paragraph break
- motivate an axis or viewpoint change

## 7. Decide Shot Size

Use these defaults:

- expression, micro-reaction, suspicion, inner turn -> close
- body action, gesture, tactical movement -> medium or full
- relationship in space, power layout, entrances, exits -> wide or two-shot

Then check:

- avoid same-size same-composition repetition
- avoid wide-tight-wide-tight drawer-pulling
- prefer a gradual size path when building tension or release

## 8. Decide Height and Angle

Ask:

- Should the audience feel equal, dominant, pressured, detached, or intimate?
- Is the shot aligned with a character's psychological position or an outside observer?
- Does the scene need stability or disturbance?

Use height/angle only if it helps answer those questions.

## 9. Decide Composition and Focus

For each shot, state:

- first visual focus
- supporting focus
- whether the frame feels open, closed, dense, empty, balanced, or skewed
- whether depth and layering help the beat

Do not use symmetry or asymmetry just because it looks cool.

## 10. Decide Movement and Blocking

Movement must do at least one job:

- reveal information
- transfer focus
- change power or distance
- increase or release tension
- justify a cut or a new angle

If a move does none of these, remove or simplify it.

## 11. Check Cut Logic

For every cut, answer:

- what new thing does this cut give the audience?
- why is this the correct next image instead of another one?
- does the cut follow eye direction, motion, rhythm, or meaning?

If the answer is weak, the cut is weak.

## 12. Output Format

Default table:

`Shot # | Beat | Purpose | Viewpoint | Framing/Size | Height/Angle | Composition/Focus | Blocking/Movement | Cut In/Out Logic`

Add notes only when they change execution.

## 13. Constraint Mode

If the user asks for a low-budget or easy-to-shoot board:

- reduce setup count
- favor reusable axes
- favor stronger main shots over extra coverage
- collapse redundant reaction shots
- keep movement motivated but simple
